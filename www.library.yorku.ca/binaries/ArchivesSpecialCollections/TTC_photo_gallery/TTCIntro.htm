Celebrates the 50th Anniversary of Canada's First Subway

 

* celebrations for the arrival of the subway at York are still pending. York University Archives hopes that we will be marking its first anniversary before the University turns fifty!

On Tuesday, 30 March 1954, approximately 5000 people gathered outside the Davisville Subway Station to watch Premier Leslie Frost and Toronto Mayor Allan Lamport throw the ceremonial switch that would mark the official opening of Canada's first subway. After five years of construction and at a cost of $59, 000, 000, the first trains left the twelve stations on the Toronto Transit Commission's new Yonge Line at 1:30 PM.  

The Yonge Line spanned a distance of 4.6 miles from Davisville to Union Station. Earlier in the day, the TTC tested the speed of its subway and covered the distance in twelve minutes. Although the trains would run at much slower speeds when they carried passengers, the travel time would be a significant improvement on the streetcars that previously traveled along a similar route. The opening of the subway meant that the streetcars would make their last journeys on the same day.  

During the first half hour of operation, token machines at various subway stations were mistakenly dispensing eight tokens for a quarter. After the technical glitches were corrected, the machines correctly dispensed three tokens for the same price making the price of a single fair at slightly more than eight cents. 

In honour of the fiftieth anniversary of the opening of the Yonge Line, York University Archives and Special Collections is pleased to present this photo gallery, which high lights some of the day's activities. These photos were part of a special 22-page souvenir section of the Toronto Telegram in 1954 and can now be found from the Toronto Telegram photograph collection housed here at York.  To order scans of any of the photos on this website, contact York University Archives.
